# Visual Description Craft

How to write rich, specific, filmable prose that replaces a sketched storyboard frame. The reader must be able to *see* the frame in their head, and a director must be able to *shoot* it without asking what was meant.

## The Core Principle

A storyboard frame, written as text, has one job: **make the image inevitable**. After reading the description, a competent director, DP, casting director, and stylist should converge on roughly the same picture — not identical, but unambiguous in tone, action, and meaning.

The Heath brothers' principle from *Made to Stick* applies directly: **be concrete**. *Concrete is what allows people to coordinate.* "A man feels nostalgic" is abstract. "A man stands at the kitchen sink in a faded Pearl Jam t-shirt, holding a chipped mug, watching steam fog the window where his son's stick-figure drawing is still taped from ten years ago" is concrete. Crew can shoot the second one.

## The Six Dimensions of a Frame

Every frame's description should address most of these. Skip a dimension only when intentionally absent:

| Dimension | Question to Answer |
|-----------|-------------------|
| **Place** | Where, exactly, in what kind of space, with what character |
| **Time** | Hour, season, weather, light direction |
| **People** | Who, how many, what they look like, what they're doing |
| **Action** | What is moving, in what direction, at what speed |
| **Mood** | What this frame *feels* like — emotional register |
| **Detail** | The one specific thing that makes this frame *this* and not generic |

The "detail" is the most important and the easiest to skip. Sullivan in *Hey Whipple*: the great spots are great because of *one specific thing*.

## Specificity Ladder

Bad → Good → Great:

| Generic | Specific | Filmable |
|---------|----------|----------|
| A kitchen | A small apartment kitchen | A galley kitchen in a 1970s walkup, mustard-yellow tile splashback, single window |
| A woman | A woman in her 30s | A woman, late 30s, hair scraped into a half-bun, paint on her left forearm |
| It's morning | Morning light | Soft east-window light, dust motes visible in the beam |
| She's tired | She looks exhausted | Heavy under-eyes, a long blink, a hand pressed flat to the counter for balance |
| A car | A red car | A 1992 Volvo wagon, salt-stained, one hubcap missing |
| She drinks coffee | She sips her coffee | She wraps both hands around the mug, blows once, stares past it at nothing |

The right rung is the highest one the duration and tone can carry. A 6s bumper rarely earns a Volvo's missing hubcap; a 90s cinema spot does.

## Sensory Layering

Visual frames are predominantly sight — but text storyboards can layer in *implied* sound, smell, texture, and temperature. The implication helps the reader feel the frame, and it informs sound design.

- **Sight (primary)** — composition, light, color, motion
- **Implied sound** — *the kettle is whistling, half-acknowledged* / *a fluorescent ballast hums*
- **Implied texture** — *cracked vinyl banquette* / *cold matte aluminum*
- **Implied temperature** — *breath visible* / *back of his neck is wet*
- **Implied smell** — *air thick with garlic and butane* (rarely belabored — a single beat goes far)

Layer 2–3 senses per frame at most. More becomes purple.

## Cast & Casting Notes

For each principal subject, give:

- **Age range** (precise: "late 20s" beats "young")
- **Gender presentation**
- **Ethnicity** — when relevant to story; default open unless specified
- **Body / Stature** — height, build, posture
- **Distinctive feature** — one thing the camera will land on (a gap-tooth, a pianist's hands, a scar, sun-faded tattoos, a perpetual smirk)
- **Energy** — *the kind of person who arrives fifteen minutes early; the kind of person who'd argue with a parking meter; the kind of person who hasn't been asked their opinion in a while*
- **Reference** — sparingly: *"Henry Golding meets a younger Mahershala Ali"* / *"the energy of Phoebe Waller-Bridge in* Fleabag*"*

Cast for *truth* over polish. Real-feeling humans almost always outperform models.

## Wardrobe

Wardrobe carries class, era, season, profession, and self-image.

- **Specifics** — "a faded Carhartt jacket over a navy thermal, paint-flecked Dickies, scuffed brown Red Wings"
- **Materiality** — soft, stiff, glossy, matted, lived-in, brand-new
- **Fit** — oversized, tailored, slightly small, deliberately ill-fitting
- **Era / Reference** — "1990s prep gone to seed" / "early Aughts indie sleaze" / "post-COVID quiet luxury"
- **Continuity flags** — note what stays the same across frames (the same coat carries the character)

Avoid the cliché ad uniform: anonymous chinos and a well-cut white tee. It's why so many ads look like they were shot on the same day in the same Brooklyn loft.

## Location

Locations are characters. Describe them as such:

- **Type and scale** — a 12-stool diner, a 40,000-sq-ft fulfillment warehouse, a single-room cabin
- **Architectural era** — postwar tract home, Brutalist concrete plaza, glass-curtain office tower
- **State of repair** — pristine / lived-in / decaying / freshly renovated
- **Inhabitation cues** — what objects tell you about who uses the space (a cracked Yelp! sticker on a register, a takeout menu pinned with a magnet, a single yellow Post-it on a monitor)
- **Time-of-day signature** — the same diner reads completely different at 7am vs. 2pm vs. 11pm

## Light

Direction × quality × color × source.

- **Direction** — front / side / back / top / under
- **Quality** — hard / soft / diffused / dappled / specular
- **Color** — warm tungsten / cool daylight / sodium-orange streetlight / fluorescent green-cyan / firelight amber / monitor blue
- **Source** — motivated by what (window, lamp, sun, neon, screen)
- **Contrast ratio** — flat / low contrast / high contrast / chiaroscuro

*"Single-source side light from a high south-facing window, hard, clipping the right cheekbone, fill from a white wall opposite — a Vermeer-leaning frame."*

---

## Color

Anchor the palette in 2–3 dominant tones plus an accent.

- **Dominant** — fills most of the frame
- **Secondary** — wardrobe or set dressing
- **Accent / Pop** — small but eye-magnetizing (a brand red, a single yellow taxi, a green emergency exit sign)
- **Skin tones** — flatly noted: "warm, golden-graded skin" / "cool, neutral, true-to-life"

Optional grade reference: *"think* Lost in Translation *Tokyo at night — sodium / mercury-vapor / neon, blacks lifted."*

## Motion in Frame

Storyboards live or die on *what is moving*. Describe motion as a vector.

- **Subject motion** — who/what is moving, in what direction, at what speed (a slow turn, a sprint past camera, a hand drifting up to the face)
- **Camera motion** — see Shot Language reference
- **Internal motion** — wind in fabric, steam, dust, water, traffic, leaves
- **Stillness** — when nothing moves, name it. *"Held still — only her chest rises."*

Adjacent frames should show *change*. If two frames have the same motion and same composition, one is redundant (Cristiano).

## Reference Anchors

Use named references sparingly to set texture. Two well-chosen anchors per spot beat ten.

- **Filmic reference** — *"the 16mm warmth of* Moonrise Kingdom*"* / *"the cold geometry of* Foundation*"*
- **Photographic reference** — *"Stephen Shore mid-American color"* / *"Vivian Maier street"* / *"Tim Walker fashion-fairytale"*
- **Director / DP reference** — *"a Roger Deakins backlit silhouette"* / *"Wes Anderson center-punch symmetry"*
- **Painterly** — *"Hopper diner light"* / *"Caravaggio underlit"*
- **Cultural** — *"early-2000s prosumer DV-cam home video"* / *"BBC nature-doc reverence"*

Avoid stacking too many — a frame referenced from four different worlds reads as confused, not eclectic.

## Common Failure Modes

| Symptom | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| **Generic adjectives** ("beautiful," "stunning," "amazing") | Replace with the specific thing that earns the adjective |
| **Stage direction without intent** ("she walks across the room") | Add the *why* of the move — what is she crossing toward, away from, looking at |
| **Mood-without-image** ("a sense of warmth and connection") | Translate into the concrete object or action that carries the feeling |
| **Overstuffing** — three actions, four characters, two locations in one frame | Split into two frames or cut to the one moment that carries the beat |
| **Cliché stack** — diverse hands, slow-mo high-five, sunset, generic uplifting piano | Smoke it out. Replace with one specific, true detail |
| **Reference avalanche** | Cap at two references per frame |
| **Telling not showing** ("she realizes…") | Translate into the *action* that shows the realization (a hand stops mid-gesture, a long blink, a turn back to look) |

## Worked Examples

### Generic version

> Frame 3 — Wide shot of a young woman in her kitchen looking happy as she opens the package. The morning light is nice.

### Filmable version

> **Frame 3** — `00:06–00:09`
> **Shot:** Static MS, eye-level, 35mm.
> **Visual:** A galley kitchen, late summer morning. Soft east-window light raking across a butcher-block counter — dust motes catching in the beam. *Maya, 31, half-asleep,* in an oversized Sun Ra concert tee and sleep shorts, hair piled in a topknot held by a pencil. She's standing barefoot on cool tile, holding the [BRAND] box at arm's length like she's not yet decided about it. A cat tail flicks past in the background, out of focus. The room is muted — sage cabinetry, oat-toned walls, a single yellow ceramic mug on the counter as a color pop. Camera holds. She tilts her head. A small private smile begins, then she stops it before it finishes.
> **SFX:** Coffee machine sputtering quietly off-screen, a bird outside.
> **Note:** This is the "save the cat" moment — we like Maya before the brand even speaks.

The second one can be cast, scouted, lit, costumed, and shot.
