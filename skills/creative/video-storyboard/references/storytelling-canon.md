## Donald Miller — Building a StoryBrand

The customer is the hero. The brand is the guide. Cast right or the story collapses.

**The 7-Part Framework (SB7):**

1. **A Character (the customer) wants something** — name the desire in customer language, not feature language
2. **Has a problem** — external (practical), internal (emotional), philosophical (why this is wrong)
3. **Meets a guide (the brand)** — guide shows empathy + authority
4. **Who gives them a plan** — a 3-step path or an agreement that removes risk
5. **And calls them to action** — direct CTA + transitional CTA
6. **That helps them avoid failure** — name the stakes if they don't act
7. **And ends in success** — show the transformed state, the new identity

**Application to video:** open on the hero's tension (1+2), introduce the brand as the guide (3), reveal the simple plan (4), call to action (5), close with the transformed life (7). Failure (6) is the implicit "what if you don't" undercurrent.

## Joseph Campbell / Christopher Vogler — The Hero's Journey

Campbell's monomyth (1949) compressed by Vogler into 12 stages for screen storytelling.

**The 12 Stages:**

1. Ordinary World
2. Call to Adventure
3. Refusal of the Call
4. Meeting the Mentor
5. Crossing the Threshold
6. Tests, Allies, Enemies
7. Approach to the Inmost Cave
8. The Ordeal
9. Reward
10. The Road Back
11. Resurrection
12. Return with the Elixir

**Application to video:** Apple, Nike, Disney, Patagonia, Dove use this. For a 60s+ brand film, compress to: Ordinary World → Call → Mentor (brand) → Ordeal → Transformation → Return. The brand is the mentor figure, not the hero. Hero finishes carrying the elixir — the product or its benefit — back into the ordinary world.

## Blake Snyder — Save the Cat

A 15-beat structure built on the 3-act spine. Coined the "save the cat" device — show the hero do something likable early so the audience commits.

**The 15 Beats (compressed for ad use):**

1. Opening Image — establish tone, mood, world
2. Theme Stated
3. Setup
4. Catalyst — the call
5. Debate
6. Break Into Two — commitment to the new world
7. B Story — relationship/heart line
8. Fun and Games — "the promise of the premise"
9. Midpoint — false victory or false defeat
10. Bad Guys Close In
11. All Is Lost
12. Dark Night of the Soul
13. Break Into Three — resolution
14. Finale
15. Final Image — opposite of opening, showing change

**Application to video:** for a 30s spot, collapse to Opening Image / Catalyst / Fun and Games / Midpoint / Finale / Final Image. The Opening Image and Final Image are the most strategically important — they bookend transformation. "Save the cat" beat = a moment in the first 5 seconds that makes the audience root for the hero (or the brand).

## Pixar / Emma Coats — 22 Rules of Storytelling

Story-artist field notes. The most useful for ad work:

- **#1** — Audience admires a character for *trying* more than for succeeding.
- **#2** — Keep in mind what's interesting to the *audience*, not what's fun to write.
- **#4 — The Story Spine:** *"Once upon a time there was ___. Every day, ___. One day ___. Because of that, ___. Because of that, ___. Until finally ___."* Best 15–30s ad skeleton ever written.
- **#6** — What's your character good at / comfortable with? Throw the polar opposite at them.
- **#9** — When stuck, list what would *not* happen next. Often the answer is right after.
- **#13** — Give characters opinions. Passive heroes are poison.
- **#14** — Why must you tell *this* story? That conviction is the fuel.
- **#15** — If you were your character in this situation, how would you feel? Honesty lends credibility.
- **#19** — Coincidences to get characters *into* trouble are great. Coincidences to get them *out* are cheating.
- **#22** — What's the essence of your story? The most economical telling of it? Start from there.

**Application to video:** the Story Spine (#4) is the fastest route to a 15s or 30s emotional arc. Rules 6, 13, 19 are diagnostic for second-draft tightening.

## Chip & Dan Heath — Made to Stick

Why ideas are remembered. The SUCCESs framework:

| Letter | Principle | Application to a Spot |
|--------|-----------|----------------------|
| **S** | Simple | One core idea — the SMP. Strip everything that isn't it. |
| **U** | Unexpected | Break a pattern in the first 3 seconds. Schema violation = attention. |
| **C** | Concrete | Sensory, specific, filmable. "A Cadbury chocolate gorilla drumming to Phil Collins" — not "joy." |
| **C** | Credible | Demonstration, statistic in context, expert source, or an antiauthority who's lived it. |
| **E** | Emotional | Make them feel something for a *person*, not for an abstraction. Mother Teresa principle. |
| **S** | Stories | Wrap the idea in narrative — Springboard, Connection, or Challenge plot. |

**Three Stock Plots:**
- **Challenge plot** — underdog, overcoming odds. Default for sports, fitness, education.
- **Connection plot** — bridge across difference. Default for inclusion, family, community.
- **Creativity plot** — solve a puzzle, break a constraint. Default for tech, design, R&D.

**Application to video:** before drafting frames, audit the concept against all six SUCCESs criteria. The weakest one is the one to strengthen.

## Luke Sullivan — Hey Whipple, Squeeze This

The bible for modern advertising creatives. Operative principles:

- **Conflict is always more interesting.** Setups without tension are wallpaper.
- **The product is the hero of the story, but not the protagonist.** The customer's life is. The product enables the change.
- **Authenticity over polish.** Real beats slick. Polish without truth reads as advertising.
- **Simplicity is a virtue.** One idea per spot. Strip ruthlessly.
- **Visual ideas beat verbal ideas.** A great spot can be paraphrased as a single image with a twist.
- **The Whipple phenomenon** — repetition and irritation can sell, but they cost brand equity. Don't aspire there.
- **Two-world structure** — Before/After, Them/Us, World-A/World-B is the most reliable comparative skeleton.
- **Earn the headline.** The end frame / tagline must pay off the setup, not just label it.
- **Clichés to smoke out:** diverse hands stacking, slow-mo group laugh, generic uplifting piano, sunset silhouettes, "we believe…" manifesto unless the manifesto is *specific* and *true*.
- **Find the human truth first.** The insight under the brief. Without it, you're decorating.

## David Ogilvy — Ogilvy on Advertising

The empirical school. Ogilvy ran his copy past data and made rules from what worked. Operative for video:

- **What you show beats what you say.** The spot should sell with the sound off.
- **Show the product. Show the package.** Spots that end on the pack change brand preference more than spots that don't.
- **Mention the product multiple times** — at the start, in the middle, on the end frame.
- **Build a sharply defined brand personality.** The most distinctive personality wins the largest share at the highest profit.
- **Tell the truth.** "Never write an advertisement which you wouldn't want your own family to read."
- **Facts sell.** Specific, concrete benefit beats vague aspiration.
- **Test, don't argue.** Treat creative judgment as hypothesis.
- **Hooks belong at the start.** Open with the strongest visual or claim, not a slow build.

## Giuseppe Cristiano — The Storyboard Artist

Practical guide to professional storyboarding for film, TV, and advertising. Operative for video work even when output is text:

- A storyboard is a *communication tool* for the crew and the client — not an art project.
- Frame *intent*, not just composition. Each frame answers: what is this moment doing for the story?
- Coverage discipline — cover the moment from establish + action + reaction + insert. Even in a 15s spot you usually need a wide, a medium, a close, and an insert.
- Show the *change* between frames. If two adjacent frames don't move the story or the camera, one is redundant.
- Match-cut thinking — design transitions into the boards, don't bolt them on.
- Insert frames for product, hand-action, packaging, screens — these are where commercial spots live or die.

## Robert McKee — Story

McKee's classical screenwriting principles distilled for ad use:

- **Story is conflict.** No conflict, no story. Period.
- **The Inciting Incident** must arrive early — for a 30s spot, by second 3.
- **The Gap** between expectation and result is where audiences engage. Subvert expectation honestly.
- **Protagonists pursue a conscious desire and an unconscious need.** The product solves both.
- **The negation of the negation** — the deepest emotional charge is in not just the absence of the good, but its corruption (joy → sadness → cruelty disguised as joy). Dramatic spots earn this when they reach for it.

## Annette Simmons — The Story Factor

For brand and corporate video. Six story types every brand uses:

1. **"Who I Am" stories** — founder origin, brand identity
2. **"Why I Am Here" stories** — motivation, mission
3. **"Vision" stories** — future-state, what we're building toward
4. **"Teaching" stories** — explainers, demos, education
5. **"Values in Action" stories** — proof through behavior
6. **"I-Know-What-You're-Thinking" stories** — surface and disarm objections

**Application to video:** for B2B, brand, recruiting, and founder-led content, pick the type up front — each has a different structural shape and a different audio register.
