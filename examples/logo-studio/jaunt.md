# Jaunt

**Product:** A premium electric-bike share operating in design districts, arts neighborhoods, and university-adjacent quarters of mid-sized cities. Unlike the blue-and-red utility-scooter operators, Jaunt runs a *small*, well-designed fleet — hand-numbered bikes, geofenced to walkable creative districts, rentable by the hour or the afternoon through a minimalist app. Partners include independent coffee shops, galleries, and record stores that host the docks.

**Category:** Urban micro-mobility / lifestyle service.

**Audience:** Urbanites between 24 and 40 — designers, students, writers, baristas, gallery-hoppers, first-date takers. They walk when they can, take transit when they must, and would rather not own a car. They're mildly allergic to the yellow-scooter aesthetic and actively annoyed by the disabled-scooter-in-the-bushes culture that came with it. They would pay a small premium for something that looks and feels good to ride.

**The real problem:** The micro-mobility category has been colonized by venture-scale operators whose design language is *fluorescent tech-adjacent utility*. The bikes are ugly, the apps are aggressive, and the urban footprint is a mess. Cities are pushing back. There is room — specifically in the "third place" neighborhoods of mid-sized cities — for an operator that behaves more like a neighborhood service (and a well-designed one) than a global platform. The brand opportunity is to make a short, idle trip around a beloved neighborhood feel like a small *occasion*.

**Competitive context:** Lime, Bird, Lyft Citi Bike, Veo (mass-transit utility). Aspirational peers sit outside the category: Rapha (cycling as belonging to a culture), Blue Bottle (neighborhood as identity), Kinfolk (the aesthetics of idle time), Muji (considered utility).

## Concept: *A Short, Good Trip*

### The Story

A *jaunt* is the English word for a short pleasure trip — usually on foot, usually unplanned, usually in good weather. The brand reclaims the word for the city. Jaunt is not a transit company; it is a company that sells short, good trips. The bike is the object; the pleasant afternoon is the product. Every piece of the experience — the fleet's color, the app's tone, the sign on the neighborhood café that hosts the dock — reminds the rider that they are doing something *small and pleasurable on purpose.*

The brand's defining discipline: it refuses to behave like a platform. It behaves like a neighborhood.

### Mood & Atmosphere

Golden-hour light bouncing off a painted stucco wall. The hiss of an espresso machine through an open door. A yellow awning, a blue sky, a gallery you didn't plan to walk into. The feeling of riding a well-tuned bike on a flat street with one hand off the bars. The color palette of a seaside town in Liguria. The graphic confidence of a 1970s Olivetti catalog.

The brand is **sunny, generous, physically precise** — a designed object you use on the way to somewhere pleasant.

### Visual Language

**Color Palette**

- **Primary — Signal Cream (`#F7F1E6`):** A warm, architectural off-white. Used as the bike's body color and the brand's ambient field. This is the color choice that rebuts the entire category — a vehicle you don't wince at.
- **Primary — Jaunt Red (`#E4543C`):** A warm, slightly orange red. The color of a garage door in a Mediterranean town, a good tomato, a vintage Vespa. Used for the logo, the saddle, the grips, the UI's primary action, and — crucially — the **geofence line** drawn on the city map. The brand's single loudest gesture, used with full commitment.
- **Secondary — Shade Navy (`#1F2E3D`):** A deep matte navy. Used for the frame accents, the rider's helmet, body copy, and photography low-key treatment. Grounds the warm palette.
- **Secondary — Terrace Olive (`#7A8B4A`):** A muted olive-green for secondary chrome, map-layer terrain, and supporting typographic accents. Borrowed from awning stripes and painted metal benches.
- **Accent — Sky Tile (`#B8D4D2`):** A pale, chalky teal. Used very sparingly, as a "good weather" tonal — progress indicators, ride-in-progress states.
- **Neutrals — three warm greys** calibrated against Signal Cream, never cool. The brand does not have a single cool grey in its entire system.

The palette reads **warm, European, and unapologetically cheerful** — a direct refusal of the neon-safety-green micro-mobility vocabulary.

**Typography**

- **Display:** A geometric sans with a warm humanist touch — something in the GT Flexa or Visuelt family, or a custom cut with slightly softened joins. Used large, confidently, often set in Jaunt Red on Signal Cream. Numbers are set as display elements — big station numbers, big route lengths, big minute counts.
- **Body/UI:** A clean neo-grotesque (Söhne Buch, Inter Display) at a generous reading size. Optimized for glanceability — most UI text is read in motion or with one hand on a bike.
- **Numerals:** A slab-adjacent monospace for ride metrics, dock numbers, and the hand-numbered unit badge on each bike. Treats numbers as a **visual feature** of the brand, not an afterthought.
- **Scale:** Bold, confident hierarchy — large display to small caption, very little in between. The brand is comfortable with empty space between a headline and a detail.

**Composition & Layout**

Swiss-international grid, loosened up. Generous use of single-color fields — a full Signal Cream screen with one Jaunt Red dot and a single line of display type is a canonical Jaunt composition. The app is built around a **map as primary interface**, with UI drawers that slide over it like pieces of paper over a desk.

Posters and print use late-modernist composition — large bold numerals, a single photograph, a three-word headline. The brand is not afraid of silence in its compositions.

**Imagery Direction**

Two deliberate modes:

1. **Product stills.** The bike shot like a designed object — cream body, red saddle and grips, against colored architectural walls in the cities where Jaunt operates. Hard light, short shadows, Palm Springs mid-century photography discipline. The bike is the hero.
2. **Riding-life photography.** Real riders, real neighborhoods, mid-afternoon. A woman with a bag of bread in the front basket outside a bakery. A pair of friends locking bikes at a gallery. Shot on film-emulating digital with a warm palette. Framed wide — the **neighborhood** is always in the shot.

No action-sport shots. No helmet-cam. No motion blur. The brand is about *arriving*, not *speed*.

**Iconography & Supporting Elements**

A custom wordmark — *jaunt* set lowercase in the display sans, with a small red tick drawn above the **u** that reads simultaneously as an accent mark, a hand-wave, and a tiny route line. The wordmark carries the whole brand: playful, designed, warm, unmistakable on a small metal badge.

Supporting icons are drawn at a single thick-geometric weight — about 2px at 24px — with rounded terminals that echo the bike's own tube profile. A custom set of neighborhood pictograms (coffee, gallery, park, bookshop) appears on the in-app map as small Jaunt Red marks against Signal Cream terrain.

Each bike is **hand-numbered** on the head tube with the slab monospace — *No. 0147* — and the brand makes visual use of this on packaging, social, and collateral. Fleet serial numbers as typography is a core brand device.

**Motion & Interaction**

Motion is **spring-based, medium-stiffness** — things arrive with a single confident overshoot and settle. Nothing in the system decelerates slowly. Unlocking a bike plays a short chime (two notes, a minor third up) and a single Jaunt Red pulse around the bike's location on the map. Ride completion shows a small red line tracing the route with a cream-paper background — the app turns the ride into a little printed keepsake.

### Application Vision

**The bike.** Cream step-through frame, matte finish. Red leather-look saddle and grips. Navy fenders and chainguard. A cork-wrapped handlebar. Front basket as standard. The wordmark lasered into the top tube; the hand-number painted on the head tube. The bike is designed to be the nicest-looking object on the sidewalk, full stop.

**App.** A full-bleed map on launch, rendered in a custom Jaunt cartography — cream base, olive parks, navy water, and the district's geofence drawn as a single Jaunt Red hairline. One primary action: **Jaunt**. Minutes-to-nearest-bike in enormous display type. After the ride: a saved card — route, duration, start dock, end dock — set as a typographic souvenir.

**Wordmark.** *jaunt* lowercase in the display sans, a small red accent tick above the **u**. Six characters, one color, zero decoration. Works at 8mm on a metal head-tube badge and as a full-bleed wall graphic at a partner café.

**Docks.** Small — six to eight bikes — installed in partnership with neighborhood hosts (bookshops, cafés, galleries, record stores). Each dock has a Signal Cream pole with a Jaunt Red band and the host's name co-set. The dock reads as part of the neighborhood, not as infrastructure imposed on it.

**Social.** Riding-life photography, bike-as-object stills, the numbered fleet, and hand-illustrated neighborhood maps printed and photographed in place. The brand's Instagram looks closer to an independent cycling magazine than to a mobility app.

**Print.** Neighborhood guides — 8-page newsprint booklets made with local editors — distributed free at dock hosts. A bike lock key fob. A cotton tote with the hand-number. A small annual printed "Year in Jaunts" card for frequent riders.

**City presence.** Each launch city gets a custom **neighborhood poster** — a single large numeral (the district's average jaunt length in minutes), a photograph of a local rider, and the wordmark. Wheatpasted in place. The brand expands by *being seen* somewhere before it's available.

### Strategic Rationale

Every design choice refuses the dominant category grammar — fluorescent, utilitarian, dump-and-go. The cream body color alone reframes what a shared bike is *for*. The hand-number on each bike, the named partner docks, the in-place newsprint guides — all of it signals that this operator behaves like a neighbor, not a platform. That's the core strategic bet: in an era when cities are skeptical of mobility operators, the ones who *behave like guests* will get the permits, the partnerships, and the favorable press.

The price premium is justified by the experience design. The brand doesn't compete with Lime on unit economics — it competes on the proposition that a short trip can be a small pleasure. That's a product category with much higher margins than "last mile transit."

### Audience Impact

The person encountering this brand thinks: *oh — someone thought about this the whole way through.* They feel slightly more like themselves when riding it. They take a photo of the bike leaned against a tiled wall. They tell their friend. Within a season, the hand-numbered bike becomes a small, unplanned emblem of the neighborhood itself.
