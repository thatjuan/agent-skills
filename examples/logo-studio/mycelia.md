# Mycelia

**Product:** Adaptogenic mushroom coffee — a daily ritual blend of arabica with lion's mane, cordyceps, and chaga extracts.

**Category:** Functional beverage / wellness CPG.

**Audience:** Knowledge workers, creative professionals, and founders between 28 and 45 who have outgrown energy drinks and nootropic gummies. They track their sleep, cap their caffeine, and would rather describe their morning as a *practice* than a *habit*. They read Oliver Burkeman, own a ceramic pour-over, and are mildly suspicious of anything that looks too "wellness."

**The real problem:** The functional-mushroom category is visually split between clinical-supplement sterility (white tubs, sans-serif confidence, milligram counts) and crunchy-granola earthiness (hand-drawn mushrooms, kraft paper, chakra adjacencies). Neither feels grown-up. The opportunity is a third territory — quiet, considered, literary — that treats the drinker like an adult.

**Competitive context:** Four Sigmatic (playful, accessible), MUD\WTR (countercultural, punky), Ryze (wellness-bro slick). Aspirational peers sit outside the category: Aesop, Le Labo, Nomos Glashütte, A24.

## Concept: *The Understory*

### The Story

Mycelia takes its name from the hidden network beneath a forest floor — the quiet intelligence that links trees, moves nutrients, and holds everything together without ever being seen. The brand positions itself the same way: a daily support system that works below the surface of an attentive life. Not a boost. Not a hack. An *understory*.

Every touchpoint treats the drinker as a thoughtful person choosing a thoughtful thing. The brand voice is that of a naturalist-editor: precise, affectionate about its subject, unembarrassed about quiet pleasure.

### Mood & Atmosphere

Morning light through a north-facing window. The weight of a hardcover book in the hand. The smell of wet bark, old paper, and something faintly sweet. An observatory at dawn. A herbarium. A kitchen where nothing is urgent.

The brand is **hushed but not sleepy**. It has the confidence of a thing that doesn't need to raise its voice. If it were a sound, it would be a single piano note held under a low field recording of wind through pine.

### Visual Language

**Color Palette**

- **Primary — Loam (`#2B241C`):** A deep, near-black brown. The color of rich soil, espresso, and old leather. Carries the earth without being muddy.
- **Primary — Parchment (`#F0EAD9`):** A warm off-white with a faint yellow cast. Aged book-paper. Never bright white — brightness is a thing this brand politely declines.
- **Secondary — Lichen (`#8A9479`):** A dusty sage-grey-green. Pulled from reindeer moss and patinated copper. Signals nature without signaling *nature*.
- **Secondary — Hen-of-the-Wood (`#7A4A2E`):** A warm rust-umber. The color of a cross-sectioned cap. Used sparingly.
- **Accent — Spore (`#D4C4A1`):** A pale sand-ochre. Reserved for highlight, foil stamping, and punctuation.
- **Neutrals — a three-step grey-brown scale** between Loam and Parchment, used for hierarchy on pack and web.

The palette deliberately avoids the wellness-category clichés of sage-millennial-green and terracotta-orange. Everything reads **herbarium, not health-store.**

**Typography**

- **Display:** A contemporary interpretation of a transitional serif — something in the lineage of Caslon or Mrs Eaves, but with sharper terminals and a touch of the modernist. Used large, tracked loose, often a single word per line. Carries the brand's literary posture.
- **Body:** A humanist sans with a warm, slightly irregular rhythm — think Söhne, GT America Mono for detail work, or a custom cut. Generous leading. Never condensed.
- **Incidental:** A monospaced serif (Pitch, or similar) used for small-caps metadata — dose, origin, batch — the way a natural-history journal labels a specimen.
- **Scale:** 1.25 ratio. Restrained hierarchy. Most pages have two type sizes, not six.

**Composition & Layout**

Editorial grid with deep margins — closer to a Penguin Classics spread than a DTC landing page. Long measure for reading text, generous whitespace as a **pacing device**, not a decorative one. Asymmetric compositions that let a single product photo or a single sentence carry an entire screen. Rules and hairlines borrowed from monograph design.

**Imagery Direction**

Two photographic modes, used in deliberate alternation:

1. **Specimen photography** — single mushrooms, single grounds, single cups, shot in diffuse north light against Parchment. Macro detail. Shadows allowed to fall long. Styling is forensic, not foodie.
2. **Human moments** — wide, quiet environmental shots. A desk. A window. A pair of hands holding a cup. Never a face full-on. Grain is permitted. Faint motion blur is permitted. The brand does not use stock people.

Film color science — slight warmth in the midtones, lifted blacks, no saturation spikes. Nothing about the imagery screams *content*.

**Iconography & Supporting Elements**

A custom mark: a monoline circular diagram inspired by cross-sections of mycelial networks and 19th-century botanical plates — a hub with radiating filaments, drawn at one hairline weight. The mark scales from 8mm (pack foil) to a full-bleed web hero without losing character.

Supporting illustrations are **botanical line art** in Lichen or Loam — never full-color illustration. Feels like it was pulled from a Victorian field guide, recut with a modernist confidence.

**Motion & Interaction**

Motion is **slow, damped, and inevitable** — 400–600ms eases, no bounce, no overshoot. Things don't pop into place; they *settle*. One signature moment: the mycelial mark animating into its full form on page load, filament by filament, over roughly two seconds. It's the brand's only piece of theater. Everything else holds still.

### Application Vision

**Website.** A single long column on the homepage. A full-bleed specimen photograph of the blend, held for a beat. Below it, a single sentence set in the display serif: *"A quiet support for long-attention work."* Product detail pages read like a monograph entry — origin, compound, dose, sourcing notes, brewing instructions — each in its own clearly-labeled section. No testimonials carousel. No "as seen in." A journal section called **Field Notes** publishes slow essays on fungi, focus, and working life.

**Packaging.** Matte Parchment kraft, soft-touch laminate, single foil-stamped mycelial mark in Loam. Ingredient panel set in the monospaced serif, small caps, left-aligned — the way a specimen label is set. The mushroom species named on the front like a book title, not a marketing claim. Weight and dose stated plainly.

**Wordmark.** The word **mycelia** set lowercase in the display serif, letters tracked slightly loose, a single hairline rule underneath. The brand's restraint is encoded in the logo itself.

**Social.** Almost no product-hero shots. Mostly macro specimens, pages from vintage mycology books, typographic quotes from essayists the audience already reads. Low post cadence. Zero emoji.

**Physical.** A flagship brewing ritual kit — ceramic cup (unglazed interior), linen brew bag, specimen card — packaged in a clothbound box that reads like a pocket field guide. Retail presence in independent bookshops and specialty coffee bars before supplement stores.

### Strategic Rationale

Every choice here is a *decline*. Declining bright green signals the brand isn't chasing the wellness category. Declining sans-serif confidence signals it isn't chasing the supplement category. Declining testimonial walls and influencer shorthand signals it treats its audience as readers, not conversion targets. The literary register — serif display, long measure, field-note voice — borrows equity from a world the target audience already trusts (quality publishing, specialty coffee, independent perfumery) and transplants it into a category that has not yet been served that way.

### Audience Impact

The person encountering this brand thinks: *this was made by someone who takes their subject seriously.* They feel permission to take their own morning seriously. They buy it, and they re-order — not because of a retention funnel, but because it has become part of how they think about their day.
